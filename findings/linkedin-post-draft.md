# LinkedIn Post Draft — Missouri Findings

**Status: DRAFT — for Russ's review. Numbers verified against dsca.db 2026-06-09.**

---

Up to 40% of seizures in people with dementia are silent. No convulsions. No shaking.
They look like a blank stare. Sudden confusion. An agitated evening.

Recognizing one takes a trained eye, watching closely, with time to document what they saw.

So I asked a simple question: **how often do those conditions actually exist in a nursing home?**

I took the public inspection records for every certified nursing home in Missouri — all 487 — and ran them through the Dementia Seizure Spectrum™ Framework, the clinical taxonomy I built during my doctoral research.

What the public record shows:

→ **9 of 487.** That's how many facilities have a clean record on every warning condition we screen for. The other 478 carry at least one.

→ **28 minutes.** The average registered-nurse time per resident, per day. The national benchmark is 45 minutes. The trained eye that would catch a silent seizure is barely in the building.

→ **The trained eye keeps leaving.** At more than half of facilities, nursing staff turnover exceeds 50% a year.

→ **Nearly half** (46%) have been cited repeatedly for failing to prevent accidents and falls — and an unwitnessed fall is one of the ways a missed seizure surfaces in a chart.

To be clear about what this is and isn't:
No facility is named. These are screening signals from public CMS data, classified through a disclosed method — not findings that any facility missed a seizure, and not a judgment of anyone's care.

But the pattern is the point. The conditions under which silent seizures go unrecognized aren't rare. In Missouri, they're the norm.

Missouri is the first state. The framework is built to run on all of them.

If you work in aging care, senior housing, or dementia research and want to see the method — it's open. That's the point of a standard.
