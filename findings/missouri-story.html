Most seizures in dementia are silent.
We measured how easy they are to miss.

Up to 40% of seizures in people with dementia produce no convulsions — they look like a blank stare, sudden confusion, an agitated evening. Catching one takes a trained eye, watching closely, with time to write down what they saw. So we asked a simple question: how often do those conditions actually exist?

What We Did

Every nursing home in Missouri, one clinical lens.

We took the public inspection records for all 487 certified nursing homes in Missouri — the same CMS data anyone can look up, covering each facility's three most recent state inspections (reaching back to 2019 where COVID delayed surveys) plus current staffing and rating data — and screened them through the Dementia Seizure Spectrum™ Framework, a clinical taxonomy for how seizures actually present in dementia.

The screening looks for warning conditions: repeat citations for the failures that let subtle neurological events slip past — accident prevention, medication management, professional documentation standards, behavioral-health staff training — plus the staffing levels that determine whether anyone is watching closely enough to notice.

No facility was visited. No facility is named. The rules are fixed and disclosed — the same test, applied the same way, to every building in the state.

What the Public Record Shows

Four numbers tell the story.

9 of 487
Facilities with a clean screen.
Just 9 nursing homes in Missouri show none of the warning conditions — no repeat citations in any screened category, staffing at benchmark, no enforcement flags. The other 478 carry at least one.
28 minutes
Average registered-nurse time per resident, per day.
The national benchmark is 45 minutes. The RN is the trained eye most likely to recognize a silent seizure for what it is — and on average, that eye has 28 minutes per resident per day. Nine out of ten Missouri facilities fall below the benchmark.

56%
The trained eye keeps leaving.
At more than half of Missouri facilities, nursing staff turnover exceeds 50% a year. Recognizing that a resident's blank stare is new — different from last month — requires staff who knew the resident last month.
46%
Cited repeatedly for failing to prevent accidents and falls.
Nearly half the state's facilities carry two or more citations in the accident-prevention category. Why that matters here: an unwitnessed fall is one of the most common ways a missed seizure shows up in a chart — recorded as the injury, not the event that caused it.
Why It Matters

The conditions for missing a silent seizure aren't rare. They're the norm.

None of this says any facility missed a seizure. What it says is that the conditions under which silent seizures go unrecognized — thin RN coverage, high turnover, documented gaps in the practices that catch subtle events — are present, in the public record, across nearly the entire state.

If you have a parent in care

If you operate or work in aging care
The gap is trainable.
Staff aren't failing — they were never taught that a blank stare can be a neurological event. A recognition framework and a documentation protocol close most of this gap without a single new hire.
If you invest in senior housing
This risk is visible before close.
Every warning condition in this analysis came from public data that exists for every facility in the country — which means it can be screened before capital is committed, not discovered after.
Honest Boundaries

What this is — and what it isn't.

It is a screening of public regulatory records through a disclosed clinical framework, reported only in aggregate.

It is not a finding that any facility missed a seizure — public inspection data can't establish that. It is not a judgment of any facility's care, and no facility is named. It is not a reason to panic about a loved one's facility — it's a reason to ask better questions. The full methodology, including everything this analysis cannot establish, is published openly. That's the point of a standard: anyone can check the work.
